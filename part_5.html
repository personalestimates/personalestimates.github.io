>‚ÄúIT‚ÄôS REVIVED!‚Äù: THE WEB REVIVAL MOVEMENT

An introduction to something that could be, and should be.


In a small corner of the internet, known by those that are ingrained in this virtual reality as the peripheral web (the internet outside of the usual main websites we are familiar with), rests an ever growing movement that is presenting the idea of returning back to a simpler time upon the internet; a time where the web was a place of creativity and exploration, of showing yourself, whilst keeping yourself safe. This movement aimed to combine a lot of different aspects of the web to make it a much more fun space again.


  • The freedom of customization that was brought forth by Web 1.0
  • The accessibility and technological advancements that were introduced by Web 2.0
  • And finally the proposal of anonymity and having your privacy controlled by you, and you alone that Web 3.0 was hoping for.

With all of these combined, the aptly titled the ‚ÄúWeb Revival Movement‚Äù was born.


It is not entirely clear when this movement began, but the most noted beginning to the movement was in February 2021 when a Discord (popular community chatting program) server was created as a starting point for this community and organization referred to as Yesterweb. Their main goals were presented through a long manifesto explaining that their goal is to create a unified internet that is inclusive and open for personal-growths through selflessness. Of course, this a very simplified version of their message, which wasn‚Äôt even entirely based on the internet itself, but based around our interactions with one another on the internet, but their core commitments as they call them show clearly what they were aiming for:

  1. The commitment to social responsibility and partisanship
  2. The commitment to collective well-being and personal growth
  3. The commitment to rehumanizing social relations and reversing the process of social alienation

To most outside of the peripheral web these tenets would feel more like lofty goals, the group were able to actually create a large community that became a webring (A collection of sites that all link to each other in some way), collections of community made zines, different servers on websites not connected to the core web (Opposite of peripheral web; where most people usually hang out), and even a forum! A big thing about the Yesterweb movement was also that they were the ones to spearhead an anti-web3 campaign back in November 2021, which was also how a lot of people from the core web began to be interested in the peripheral web and what Yesterweb was proposing overall. Though, in 2023, they would go on an indefinite hiatus due to the community growing too large for their small staff to be able to handle at this current time.

However, their server was where the term ‚ÄúWeb Revival‚Äù was actually coined and began to encompass that community that was built back in 2021 as they needed something to refer to what we know as the ‚Äúwider web crafting community‚Äù that didn‚Äôt just dump every person that made a personal website into this community, since there were still people who simply made a website because they wanted to, and not to join into this movement. Most of these communities also began to migrate from the known websites such as Twitter, Instagram, etc. and move to websites such as BlueSky, Mastodon, etc. because of them providing the functionality that they desire, whilst also being primarily open sourced and contributed by the people and not just one large corporation.

It should be noted that multiple different people all have their own interpretations of what Web Revival should encompass altogether, but a major thing that I‚Äôve noted from all of these places are these few common traits as presented by Melon of Melonland:

  • Creativity is First - Most see the ability to design, decorate and graffiti digital spaces as essential and powerful
  • The Internet is Fun - Most want the Web to be a playground that's free to explore and enjoy
  • Corporations are Boring - Most are sick of the monetisation, data abuse and endless breaches of trust in corporate culture
  • The Web is Friendly - All feel that the Web should be friendly and supportive; caring is a radical act
  • Right to Repair - They value the freedom to make, break and repair their stuff - tinkering is a form of debate and protest
  • One World Wide Web - They want free open knowledge and global connectivity, without paywalls, bubbles or borders
  • Chaotic Effort - They believe that value comes from time and effort put into projects they love for no reason other than love
  • No to Web3 - To most, cryptocurrencies, NFTs, unfairly trained AIs and buzzword tech are unwelcome and uncool
